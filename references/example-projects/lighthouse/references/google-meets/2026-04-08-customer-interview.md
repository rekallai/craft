# Customer Interview — Maria K., Owner of Vista Design Studio

**Date:** 2026-04-08
**Duration:** 32 minutes
**Interviewer:** Sam (product)
**Participant:** Maria K. — owner, 4-person design studio, 6 years in business

## Context

Maria runs a small branding agency. Currently uses QuickBooks Online + a spreadsheet for follow-ups. We're talking to her as part of MVP validation.

## Transcript (lightly edited for readability)

**Sam:** Walk me through what happened the last time a client paid you late.

**Maria:** Oh god. So this was like three weeks ago. I sent an invoice for $4,800 to a client we'd just wrapped a project with — they were thrilled, glowing reviews, the whole thing. Net 30. Day 30 comes and goes, no payment. I assume it's just a delay. Day 35, nothing. So now I have to email them. And I hate that email. I hate writing it. I draft it like four times. "Hi! Just following up on invoice #143, no rush, but…" — you know, the apologetic-but-firm thing.

**Sam:** How long does that take?

**Maria:** The email itself, maybe 15 minutes because I'm rewriting it. But it lives in my head all day. It's like a tab open in my brain. And then I send it, and they reply "oh so sorry, we'll get to it this week!" — and then they don't. So a week later I have to send another one. And now I'm second-guessing — are they mad at us? Did we do something wrong? Should I call?

**Sam:** What about the actual payment, when it finally comes in?

**Maria:** They paid via ACH. So the deposit hits my bank account, but it's labeled like "ACME CORP 8472" or whatever — totally opaque. I have to go into my bank, look at the date, look at the amount, cross-reference with my outstanding invoices in QuickBooks, and manually mark it paid. And I have to do this every single time. Sometimes the amount doesn't even match because they deducted a wire fee or something, and now I'm doing math.

**Sam:** If a tool could do one thing here, what would help most?

**Maria:** Honestly? Not making me write the follow-up email. If something could just send a polite, professional reminder on a schedule I set and forget — that alone would change my month. The reconciliation thing is also painful but I've kind of accepted it. The dunning is the part that costs me sleep.

## Signal

- **Strong:** Dunning is the #1 emotional and time burden, ranking above reconciliation. This validates the MVP ordering.
- **Strong:** ACH reconciliation is a clear secondary pain — confirms it belongs in MVP.
- **Notable:** She didn't mention invoice templates or PDF design once. Cosmetic polish is not a wedge.
- **Notable:** She uses QuickBooks Online but treats it as a system of record, not as a workflow tool. Positioning ourselves *alongside* QBO (not replacing it) may be the right frame for this segment.
